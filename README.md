# 💡 Research Philosophy
Thanks to the diverse assignments entrusted to me over the years, I have had the privilege of working on a wide spectrum of problems in signal processing, communication systems, intelligent sensing, and autonomous robotics, all connected by a common objective—Signal Processing for Information Extraction.

My professional journey began through an association with the National Technical Research Organisation (NTRO), where I was posted at ADRIN, Secunderabad, and worked on post-processing of remote sensing imagery. Since joining CSIR–Central Mechanical Engineering Research Institute (CSIR-CMERI) in 2010, my research has progressively expanded towards the development of intelligent engineering systems addressing industrial, strategic, and societal challenges. My current work encompasses autonomous robotics, underwater systems, sensing, perception, and artificial intelligence, while remaining firmly rooted in the principles of statistical signal processing.

Throughout this journey, I have observed that many mathematically elegant algorithms often face significant challenges when translated into practical engineering systems. This realization shaped my research philosophy: the most valuable solution is not merely the mathematically optimal one, but the one that balances theoretical optimality with practical realizability.

Consequently, my research focuses on developing inference procedures that are not only statistically rigorous but also computationally efficient and suitable for deployment in real-world systems. This perspective led to the development of a novel analytical framework based on order statistics for detecting known weak signals in additive non-Gaussian noise, demonstrating that substantial gains in hardware realizability can be achieved without compromising analytical rigor.

Today, my research continues to evolve at the intersection of mathematical statistics, estimation and detection theory, statistical signal processing, intelligent sensing, communication systems, and autonomous engineering. While application domains may vary—from communication systems and remote sensing to underwater robotics—the underlying motivation remains unchanged: to bridge mathematical theory with deployable technologies capable of addressing complex real-world problems.

Research Vision
> **"Mathematics provides the foundation. Signal processing extracts information. Intelligent sensing connects the physical world with computation. Engineering transforms these principles into technologies that solve real-world problems."**
